# Future-Proof Careers: Workforce Planning in the Age of AI

## Project Overview

This project analyzes 76 occupations across 10 career categories using PostgreSQL to understand how AI disruption risk varies across the workforce.

The goal was to identify which careers appear most vulnerable, which remain resilient, and what patterns emerge regarding salary, job characteristics, and risk growth over time.

---

## Business Questions

1. Which career categories face the highest AI disruption risk?
2. Which occupations appear most resilient?
3. Are higher-paying jobs less vulnerable to AI?
4. Which occupations are becoming vulnerable the fastest?
5. What characteristics appear to make careers more resilient?

## Finding 1: Administrative & Operations Roles Face the Highest Average Risk

| Category | Average AI Risk |
|-----------|-----------:|
| Administrative & Operations | 62.8 |
| Business & Finance | 56.8 |
| Legal | 54.8 |
| Marketing & Sales | 52.3 |

Administrative and Operations occupations exhibited the highest average disruption risk. Many of these jobs involve documentation, scheduling, information routing, and repetitive workflows that are increasingly automated by AI systems.

## Finding 2: Project & Product Management Shows the Lowest Average Risk

| Category | Average AI Risk |
|-----------|-----------:|
| Project & Product Management | 31.8 |
| Healthcare | 37.7 |
| Education | 38.8 |

Project and Product Management occupations showed the lowest average disruption risk. These roles emphasize prioritization, stakeholder alignment, trade-off decisions, and organizational influence, which remain difficult to automate.

## Key Takeaway

The analysis suggests that AI risk is driven less by compensation level and more by the nature of work performed. Roles focused on judgment, stakeholder management, accountability, and interpersonal trust appear more resilient than roles centered on routine information processing and content generation.
